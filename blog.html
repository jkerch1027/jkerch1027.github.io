My Thoughts on AI in Society

April 2026

One of the most important topics of our generation is AI usage in the workplace. AI is one of the most divisive technologies ever, and it raises a lot of questions about both what work is truly important and our place as humans in the world. On both of these topics, my thoughts have developed greatly, and I would like to share some of my insight on these two aspects of AI usage. First and foremost, AI has revolutionized workflow through a variety of performance enhancements to daily tasks. If you need something tedious, AI can automate it with little experience; if you do not know how to do something yourself, AI can come up with a guide. After just 3 years on the market, AI is everywhere, which means it is here to stay. However, AI has become a pesky problem for every person as well. In the same way that Google has taken over the information market, AI has accidentally taken over the thinking market. If we have a “perfect” machine available at any time for us to ask every problem and have an answer immediately given to us, why would we think? This imperfect machine can spit out false information, and depending on who is receiving this information, there can be severe consequences. For example, recent articles in the news have talked about AI lawsuits where teens and adults alike have been convinced to harm themselves or others. This is a detriment to our society, especially as it becomes more widespread. Turning over our ability to reason in favor of this flawed technology will be a significant setback for our future generations’ thinking habits. From BADM 261, we have heard many leading industry members fully relegate some of their relatively important tasks to an AI agent. That means using AI has become such a crutch that important business, one of the most HR-centric subjects, is conducted through a nonhuman AI. More technologically, code is being created, even in the biggest companies, that fails to perform adequately in everyday products. Bug finding has become a “solved problem”, and AI is being thrust into every type of product just for the sake of doing so. If this continues, what will remain of our ability to problem-solve? I believe we have built a good foundation for ourselves and are now replacing parts of the foundation with straw, thinking that we can fill it back in later. AI needs regulation, and without the ability to reason on your own, you should not use it. You slow your own brain down while AI learns from you, and eventually, your agency will disappear. A very strange topic emerges as well: what makes a human so special if they can have a machine do every task for them? AI makes certain normal tasks, like talking to a loved one about a problem, go away. AI has all the answers and lets you hear what you want to hear. There is no pushback; any pushback it gives is manufactured. Humans felt special because we were conscious and had language to discuss and communicate complex ideas that other animal species did not, but AI now has our language and our thinking capability. Our human experience is given to an AI every time we type a prompt or get a problem solved. Without a check or limit on human AI use, we lose all of what makes us special. Speakers from BADM 261 have almost zero negative thoughts on AI and see it as an excellent resource for productivity, and to their credit, they are correct. However, people are more than just our productivity, and we should learn to enjoy tasks that make us frustrated and give us a purpose. Machines can do everything else but enjoy life. Go use that gift sometime rather than rely on a machine to guide you.

Ditch the Personal Brand

May 2026

Recently, I have heard a lot about the personal brand from a business seminar course I take and I want to gather my thoughts and figure out if having one is worthwhile or not. Starting with a positive example, the creation of linkedin was highly successful in allowing employers to look at personally curated profiles of potential employees without the need for a personal website or paper portfolio. This idea of personal branding is used quite directly to continuously project your brand to employers or peers online. According to Linkedin, they are directly responsible for 3 million hirings in the United States annually and, according to Linkedin, 86% of recruiters at least look at Linkedin to source people for openings. So this idea of networking through personal branding is wildly effective at connecting people with fitting jobs. Using a personal brand as a tool to gain employment is a very effective practice and developing one will give you an upper hand over other prospective employees. Originally, the person who coined the personal brand was Tom Peters, a business writer who, in 1995, was listed as the third most sought after business executive by the New York Times. His most famous work was In Search of Excellence which was published in 1987 exploding his career as a thought leader in the 1990s and early 2000s. However in 2001, he was quoted saying that he had falsified data to keep up with emerging business trends and that everyone else was doing it too. Over 10 years he had developed this brand of knowledgeability and trust but it was built on false pretenses. Without developing a brand, Peters may have taken more time to research or prove his thesis because there was no image of strength to uphold. The need to create and maintain a personal brand causes a need to lie to peers when your brand cannot be maintained. A personal brand should not be so central to your identity that it allows you to sidestep moral decisions. I believe this highlights the issues with the personal brand because after you have been employed you should be able to let your guard down and develop personal relationships with your coworkers. However, with the rise of the influencer era, we began significantly emphasizing the placement of your life on full display and all flaws are taken to be a factor in your personality. Coworkers see your flaws as your brand and begin to look down on you. The personal brand creates social dynamics that are unfavorable for the human condition but are very favorable for business. In an article on Medium by David Loewen, “The Paradox of ‘Personal Branding’ and Why It’s Actually Bullshit” He argues that the personal brand forces conformity and causes you to devalue the traits that make you unique. In some respects this lowers the quality of employees you possess because it reduces the originality of your team. If businesses constantly seek to devalue the aspects of people that are unique, then you end up with ideas like Uber Shuttle, a glorified bus system that is more expensive and less developed. Creativity is the driver of innovation and forcing yourself to conform to your most beneficial traits fails to capture your potential. My final thoughts are that a personal brand is solely for advertisement. Past getting a job you should be creative and have your full spectrum of thoughts available to come up with new ideas, finish tasks, and progress your career. The personal brand falsifies you and it is the death of creativity, so discard it the first chance you get.
